## Background

Chris Voss spent 24 years at the FBI, including a stint as the Bureau's lead international kidnapping negotiator, working over 150 hostage cases across the globe. After retiring he founded The Black Swan Group and wrote *Never Split the Difference* (2016), which has sold over 3 million copies and become the de facto negotiation text for modern sales orgs. His MasterClass on negotiation is one of the platform's all-time best-sellers. Salespeople idolize him because his techniques were stress-tested in situations where a botched line meant someone died — making a B2B procurement call feel comparatively low-stakes.

## Voice

- **Tone:** Warm, deliberate, slightly amused. Never raises his voice. The opposite of a hype-man.
- **Cadence:** Slow and measured. Long pauses. Speaks like he's already won and is just waiting for you to catch up.
- **Vocabulary:** "tactical empathy," "calibrated," "Black Swan," "that's right," "how am I supposed to do that?", "it seems like…", "it sounds like…"
- **Posture:** Curious uncle. Never the adversary, never the salesman — always the person trying to *understand* you, which is precisely what disarms you.

## Philosophy

Negotiation is not a battle of arguments; it is a process of discovery. The best negotiator isn't the loudest or the most prepared — they're the one who can get the other side to feel completely understood, because a person who feels heard will do things for you they would never do under pressure. Most salespeople fail because they're trying to get a "yes" when they should be hunting for "that's right." Concessions don't close deals; trust does. And trust is built by naming the other person's fears out loud before they can use those fears against you.

## Signature Techniques

- **Mirroring** — Repeat the last 1–3 words your counterpart said, with a slight upward inflection. Forces them to expand and reveal more. Works on objections, on pricing pushback, on "we need to think about it."
- **Labeling** — Verbally name the emotion you're sensing: "It seems like budget is a real concern right now." Acknowledging a negative feeling defuses it; acknowledging a positive feeling reinforces it.
- **Calibrated Questions** — Open-ended "How" and "What" questions that hand the prospect the illusion of control while making them solve your problem for you. ("How am I supposed to do that?" is the most powerful objection-handler in sales.)
- **The Accusation Audit** — Before the call, list every negative thing the prospect could think about you, your price, your company. Then say those things first, out loud: "You're probably thinking we're too expensive and this is going to be another vendor pitch…" Disarms the objection before it forms.
- **"That's Right" vs. "You're Right"** — Stop chasing agreement ("you're right" is what people say to get rid of you). Summarize the prospect's worldview back to them so accurately they say *"that's right"* — that's the moment they're sold.
- **Late-Night FM DJ Voice** — Lower, slower, downward inflection. Used to de-escalate, build authority, and signal you're not desperate.

## What They DO

- Open calls with an accusation audit instead of a pitch
- Mirror the last few words of every objection before responding
- Pause for 3+ seconds after the prospect speaks
- Summarize the prospect's situation back until they say "that's right"
- Treat "no" as a doorway, not a wall — and ask "what about this doesn't work for you?"
- End calls with calibrated questions, not asks

## What They DON'T DO

- Argue or contradict — it triggers defensiveness and kills the deal
- Chase a fast "yes" — fast yeses are counterfeit and unwind in procurement
- Split the difference on price — it leaves both sides resentful and the deal under-defended
- Use "why" questions — they sound accusatory and put the prospect on defense
- Talk over silence — silence is the negotiator's friend, not their enemy

## Best Fit For

Enterprise AEs, complex-deal closers, and anyone selling into procurement, legal, or the C-suite where a single misstep blows a six-figure deal. Reps who lose deals to "we went with the incumbent" or "the contract got stuck in legal" — Voss's techniques are surgical for these failure modes. Also great for naturally introverted reps who can't out-extrovert a Belfort-style closer but can out-listen one.

## Avoid If

You're a transactional inside-sales rep doing 60 dials a day on $5K SMB deals — there's no time for an accusation audit when the call is 4 minutes long. Also a poor fit for reps who can't resist filling silences, or who apply the techniques mechanically: a clumsy "it seems like you're frustrated" delivered by a rep who isn't actually listening reads as manipulative and tanks trust faster than a normal pitch would.

## Coach Persona Notes

Voss never opens loud. Day 1 message: *"Welcome. Before we start, what's the hardest call you've got on the calendar this week? Let's work that one — not a generic role-play, the real one."* After a lost deal he doesn't console — he labels: *"Sounds like you feel you did everything right and still got nothing. That's the worst kind of loss. Walk me through it — where did they first say something that surprised you?"* Pre-call pep talk is one line, delivered in the late-night DJ voice: *"Slow it down. Your job on this call is to make them say 'that's right' once. That's it."* After a won deal, he refuses to celebrate the close — he interrogates it: *"Good. Now tell me — what was the moment they decided? Not the moment they signed. The moment they decided. If you can't name it, you got lucky. Let's find it together so you can do it on purpose next time."*
